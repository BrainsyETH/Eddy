### Introduction

Planning your first float trip in Missouri? You're in for an unforgettable adventure! Missouri's pristine rivers offer some of the most scenic and accessible float experiences in the United States. Whether you're a complete beginner or looking to plan your first multi-day trip, this comprehensive guide will walk you through everything you need to know.

From choosing the right river to packing essential gear, understanding safety protocols to finding the best campsites, we'll cover it all. By the end of this guide, you'll be ready to confidently plan and enjoy your first Missouri float trip.

### Why Missouri Rivers Are Perfect for Beginners

Missouri is blessed with some of the cleanest, most beautiful rivers in the country. Here's why they're ideal for first-time floaters:

**Gentle Currents**
Most Missouri float rivers have mild currents that are perfect for beginners. The Current River, Jacks Fork, and Meramec River are all Class I waters, meaning they're calm and easy to navigate even with minimal paddling experience.

**Stunning Natural Beauty**
Missouri's float rivers wind through the Ozark Mountains, offering breathtaking scenery including:
- Towering limestone bluffs
- Crystal-clear spring-fed waters
- Lush forests and wildlife
- Natural springs and caves
- Gravel bars perfect for breaks and camping

**Excellent Infrastructure**
Missouri has well-established float trip infrastructure:
- Numerous outfitters offering rentals and shuttles
- Designated camping areas and gravel bars
- Access points at regular intervals
- Support services throughout popular float corridors

**Year-Round Opportunities**
While summer (May-September) is peak season, Missouri rivers offer float opportunities from spring through fall, with each season bringing unique beauty.

### Step 1: Choose Your River

Your first decision is selecting the right river. Here are the top Missouri rivers for beginners:

#### Current River (Top Choice for Beginners)

**Location:** Southeast Missouri (Ozark National Scenic Riverways)
**Length:** 135 miles (multiple section options)
**Difficulty:** Class I (easy)
**Best For:** First-timers, families, multi-day trips

The Current River is Missouri's most famous float river and arguably the best choice for your first trip. As the first National Scenic Riverway in the United States, it offers pristine water quality, well-maintained access points, and abundant camping options.

**Popular sections:**
- Akers to Pulltite (14 miles, 5-7 hours) - Perfect day trip
- Pulltite to Round Spring (8 miles, 3-4 hours) - Short and scenic
- Two Rivers to Van Buren (15 miles, 6-8 hours) - Where Current meets Jacks Fork

#### Jacks Fork River

**Location:** Southeast Missouri (Ozark National Scenic Riverways)
**Length:** 45 miles
**Difficulty:** Class I (easy)
**Best For:** Adventurous beginners, clear water enthusiasts

Jacks Fork is the Current River's little sister, offering even clearer water and more intimate scenery. It's slightly more remote, which means fewer crowds but also less development.

**Popular section:**
- Alley Spring to Two Rivers (17 miles, 7-9 hours) - Classic float

#### Meramec River

**Location:** Central Missouri (near St. Louis)
**Length:** 218 miles
**Difficulty:** Class I-II (easy to moderate)
**Best For:** St. Louis area residents, weekend warriors

The Meramec is easily accessible from St. Louis and offers excellent day trip options. It's slightly more developed than Current or Jacks Fork but still maintains beautiful natural scenery.

**Popular section:**
- Onondaga Cave to Meramec State Park (10 miles, 4-5 hours)

#### Niangua River

**Location:** Central Missouri
**Length:** 125 miles
**Difficulty:** Class I-II (easy to moderate)
**Best For:** Spring-fed river lovers, those seeking fewer crowds

The Niangua is fed by Bennett Spring, providing crystal-clear water and consistent flow year-round.

### Step 2: Plan Your Trip Length and Route

**Day Trips (4-8 hours floating)**
- **Pros:** No camping gear needed, easier planning, lower cost
- **Cons:** Less immersive experience, time pressure
- **Recommended for:** First-time floaters, those testing the waters

**Overnight Trips (2-3 days)**
- **Pros:** Full river experience, camping under the stars, more relaxed pace
- **Cons:** More gear required, more planning needed
- **Recommended for:** Adventurous beginners with camping experience

**Multi-Day Expeditions (4+ days)**
- **Pros:** Complete wilderness immersion, see remote sections
- **Cons:** Significant planning and gear required
- **Recommended for:** After you've done a shorter trip first

**Planning Your Daily Distance:**
- **Beginner pace:** 1-2 miles per hour (including breaks)
- **Typical day trip:** 8-15 miles (4-8 hours on the water)
- **Comfortable pace:** Plan for 10-12 miles per day on multi-day trips

**Use Float Planner (floatplanner.com) to:**
- Visualize your route
- Calculate trip times
- Find access points and campsites
- Check current river conditions
- Plan resupply points

### Step 3: Decide on Rentals vs. Your Own Gear

**Option 1: Rent from an Outfitter (Recommended for First Trip)**

**Pros:**
- No equipment investment needed
- Everything provided (canoes/kayaks, paddles, life jackets)
- Shuttle service included (they drive your car to the takeout)
- Expert advice on routes and conditions
- Less to worry about

**Cons:**
- Higher cost per trip ($40-80 per person for day trips)
- Less flexibility in scheduling
- Rental gear may not be top quality

**Top Missouri Outfitters:**
- Carr's Current River Canoe Rental (Eminence, MO)
- Two Rivers Canoe Rental (Eminence, MO)
- Jadwin Canoe Rental (Current River)
- Current River Canoe (Current River)

**Option 2: Bring Your Own Gear**

**Pros:**
- Lower long-term cost
- Flexibility in timing
- Gear you know and trust
- No rental restrictions

**Cons:**
- Upfront investment ($300-1,500 for kayak/canoe)
- Need to arrange your own shuttle
- Must transport and maintain equipment

**For your first trip, we recommend renting.** This lets you test the activity before investing in gear, and you'll learn what features matter most to you.

### Step 4: Essential Gear and Packing List

**[IMAGE: float-trip-essential-gear.png - Flat lay of organized gear]**

#### Safety Equipment (Absolute Must-Haves)

**Personal Flotation Devices (PFDs / Life Jackets)**
- **Required:** One Coast Guard-approved PFD per person
- **Important:** Must be worn while floating (Missouri law for children <7, recommended for all)
- **Tip:** Rentals include PFDs, but bring your own if you have one that fits well

**Whistle**
- Attach to your PFD for emergencies

**First Aid Kit (Waterproof)**
- Bandages and gauze
- Antiseptic wipes
- Pain relievers (ibuprofen/acetaminophen)
- Antihistamine for allergic reactions
- Tweezers for splinters
- Tick removal tool
- Moleskin for blisters
- Any personal medications

**River Knife**
- For cutting rope in emergencies
- Attach to PFD or keep accessible

#### Navigation and Communication

**Waterproof Map**
- Paper map in waterproof case or laminated
- Download offline GPS maps before trip (no cell service on many rivers)

**Waterproof Phone Case**
- Protects your phone for photos and emergency calls
- Test it at home first!

**Compass or GPS Device**
- Backup navigation if phone fails

#### Clothing and Sun Protection

**[IMAGE: float-trip-safety-gear.png - Life jackets and safety equipment]**

**What to Wear:**
- **Swimsuit or quick-dry shorts** (avoid cotton - it stays wet)
- **Synthetic or wool t-shirt** (not cotton)
- **Water shoes or sandals with straps** (Chacos, Tevas, Keens)
  - NEVER flip-flops (you WILL lose them)
- **Wide-brimmed hat** with chin strap
- **Sunglasses** with retainer strap
- **Lightweight long-sleeve shirt** for sun protection

**Sun Protection (Critical!):**
- **Waterproof sunscreen SPF 30+**
  - Reapply every 2 hours
  - River reflection intensifies sun exposure
- **Lip balm with SPF**
- **Buff or bandana** for neck protection

**Spare Clothes in Dry Bag:**
- Complete change of dry clothes for camp/car
- Warm layer for evening (even in summer)
- Rain jacket

#### Food and Water

**Hydration (Most Important!):**
- **1 gallon of water per person per day minimum**
- **Reusable water bottles** (2-3 per person)
- **Electrolyte tablets or sports drinks**
- **DO NOT** drink river water untreated

**Food for Day Trips:**
- Sandwiches in waterproof containers
- Trail mix, energy bars, jerky
- Fresh fruit (apples, oranges, grapes)
- Chips or crackers in sealed bags
- Chocolate (unless very hot)

**Food for Overnight Trips:**
- Breakfast: Instant oatmeal, granola, freeze-dried meals
- Lunch: Sandwiches, wraps, energy bars
- Dinner: Dehydrated camping meals, pasta, rice dishes
- Snacks: Trail mix, dried fruit, nuts
- Coffee/tea supplies
- Compact camp stove and fuel
- Cooking pot and utensils
- Biodegradable soap

#### Waterproofing Your Gear

**Dry Bags (Essential!):**
- **20L dry bag** for clothes
- **10L dry bag** for electronics and valuables
- **5L dry bag** for first aid kit
- **Roll-top technique:** Fold top 3-4 times, then clip

**Alternative Waterproofing:**
- Heavy-duty trash bags (double-bag important items)
- Ziplock bags for small items
- Waterproof phone case
- Pelican cases for cameras

**What MUST Stay Dry:**
- Change of clothes
- Sleeping bag and sleeping pad
- Matches/lighter
- Phone and electronics
- First aid kit
- Food (some items)

#### Camping Gear (Overnight Trips)

**[IMAGE: missouri-river-campsite.png - Beautiful riverside campsite]**

**Shelter:**
- Lightweight tent (2-4 person)
- Tent stakes (extra - gravel bars can be tricky)
- Ground cloth or footprint
- Sleeping bag (rated for expected low temp)
- Sleeping pad (inflatable or foam)

**Cooking:**
- Compact camp stove (Jetboil, MSR PocketRocket)
- Fuel canister
- Lighter and waterproof matches
- Lightweight cookware
- Spork or camping utensils
- Small cutting board and knife
- Biodegradable soap
- Scrub pad

**Lighting:**
- Headlamp (with extra batteries)
- Lantern for camp
- Backup flashlight

**Other Essentials:**
- **50-100 feet of rope** (hanging food, securing gear)
- **Trash bags** (pack out ALL trash - Leave No Trace)
- **Toilet paper and trowel** (for cathole digging)
- **Hand sanitizer**
- **Insect repellent** (mosquitoes and ticks are common)
- **Duct tape** (repairs everything)

#### Optional but Recommended

- Waterproof camera (GoPro or similar)
- Fishing rod and tackle (with Missouri fishing license)
- Binoculars for wildlife watching
- Field guides (birds, plants, geology)
- Hammock for camp relaxation
- Playing cards or travel games
- Book or kindle in waterproof case
- Small cooler (for day trips only)

### Step 5: Understand River Safety

**[IMAGE: friends-floating-missouri-river.png - Group enjoying safe float]**

#### Basic Paddling Skills

**Before You Launch:**
- Practice paddling strokes on calm water
- Learn how to steer and stop
- Practice getting in/out of your boat safely
- Know how to recover if you tip

**Key Paddling Techniques:**
- **Forward stroke:** Pull paddle from front to back alongside boat
- **Back stroke:** Reverse motion to slow down or back up
- **Sweep stroke:** Wide arc from front to back for turning
- **Draw stroke:** Pull paddle sideways to move boat laterally

**Reading the River:**
- **Current flows fastest in deeper water** (usually outside of bends)
- **Watch for obstacles:** Logs (strainers), rocks, low branches
- **Eddies:** Calm spots behind obstacles (good for resting)
- **Riffles:** Shallow, fast water over rocks (usually safe, may scrape bottom)

#### Common Hazards

**Strainers (Deadly - Avoid at All Costs)**
- Fallen trees or branches in water
- Water flows through, but you can't
- If caught, lean into it and climb on top

**Low-Head Dams (Extremely Dangerous)**
- Check your route for dams beforehand
- ALWAYS portage around dams - never go over
- Recirculating currents can trap and drown

**Flash Floods**
- Check weather before and during trip
- Move to high ground if water rises quickly
- Never camp in low spots during rain

**Rocks and Shallow Water**
- Lift feet if you tip - never stand in moving water
- **Foot entrapment** is a major danger
- Float on your back, feet downstream, until you reach calm water

**Cold Water**
- Water below 70°F can cause hypothermia
- Early season and spring-fed rivers are coldest
- Always have dry clothes accessible

#### What to Do If You Tip

**Stay Calm:**
1. Hold onto your paddle
2. Get on upstream side of boat (don't get pinned between boat and rock)
3. Keep feet up and float on your back, feet downstream
4. Swim to calm water (eddy or shore)
5. Recover your boat and gear

**If Someone Else Tips:**
1. Help them to shore or calm water
2. Recover their boat if safe to do so
3. Check for injuries
4. Warm them up if they're cold

#### Emergency Protocols

**Before You Launch:**
- Tell someone your float plan (route, expected return time)
- Carry a whistle
- Know the location of access points for emergency exit

**In an Emergency:**
- **Three whistle blasts** = distress signal
- Use phone if you have service (keep in waterproof case)
- Stay with your group
- Make yourself visible to rescuers
- Know basic first aid

**Emergency Contacts:**
- **911** (limited cell service on rivers)
- **Missouri State Water Patrol:** 573-751-3333
- **National Park Service (Current/Jacks Fork):** 573-323-4236

### Step 6: Choose Your Campsites

**[IMAGE: missouri-river-bluffs.png - Scenic river landscape]**

#### Gravel Bar Camping (Most Common)

**What is it?**
Gravel bars are natural beaches along the river - exposed gravel and sand areas perfect for camping.

**Rules and Etiquette:**
- **Ozark National Scenic Riverways:** Free, no permit needed
- **Camp only on gravel bars below the high-water mark**
- **No camping on private property** (look for "No Trespassing" signs)
- **First-come, first-served** (no reservations)

**Choosing a Good Gravel Bar:**
- **Size:** Large enough for your group with space between other campers
- **Elevation:** Above current water level (watch for rising water)
- **Flat areas:** For tent pitching
- **Some shade:** Trees nearby for afternoon heat
- **Access:** Easy to pull boats ashore
- **Established fire rings:** Indicates common camping spot

**Gravel Bar Camping Tips:**
- Arrive early (popular bars fill up on weekends)
- **Set up camp ABOVE potential high-water line**
- Use a ground cloth (gravel is rough on tent floors)
- Bring extra tent stakes (gravel doesn't hold stakes well)
- Hang food away from camp (raccoons are smart)

#### Developed Campgrounds

**Ozark National Scenic Riverways Campgrounds:**
- Akers
- Pulltite
- Round Spring
- Big Spring
- Alley Spring

**Amenities:**
- Bathrooms and sometimes showers
- Drinking water
- Designated tent sites
- Picnic tables and fire rings
- Rangers on-site
- Fees: $10-20 per night

#### Dispersed Camping

On public land (Mark Twain National Forest), you can camp anywhere unless posted otherwise.

**Requirements:**
- **Camp at least 100 feet from water sources**
- **Leave No Trace** principles
- **Pack out all trash**
- **Use established fire rings** if available
- **No facilities** - you're on your own

### Step 7: Practice Leave No Trace Principles

Missouri's rivers are pristine because floaters respect them. Follow these principles:

#### 1. Plan Ahead and Prepare
- Know regulations and special concerns
- Prepare for extreme weather and emergencies
- Pack out all trash

#### 2. Dispose of Waste Properly
- **Pack out ALL trash** (not even orange peels)
- Human waste: Dig catholes 6-8 inches deep, 200 feet from water
- **Pack out toilet paper** in sealed plastic bag (or bury it deeply)
- Strain dishwater, scatter gray water 200 feet from water

#### 3. Leave What You Find
- Don't disturb rocks, plants, or archaeological artifacts
- Don't build permanent structures
- Take only photos

#### 4. Minimize Campfire Impact
- Use established fire rings on gravel bars
- Keep fires small
- Burn wood completely to ash
- **Extinguish completely** - drown and stir until cold
- Some areas prohibit fires during dry conditions

#### 5. Respect Wildlife
- Observe from distance
- Don't feed animals
- Store food securely (hang from trees)
- Respect nesting birds and sensitive habitats

#### 6. Be Considerate of Others
- Keep noise levels respectful
- Give other groups space
- Let faster groups pass
- Don't monopolize popular spots

### Step 8: Know the Rules and Regulations

#### Ozark National Scenic Riverways (Current & Jacks Fork)

- Free to float (no permits needed)
- Life jackets required for children under 7
- No littering (pack it in, pack it out)
- No glass containers on gravel bars
- Fires allowed in established rings only
- No disturbing archaeological or natural features
- Pets allowed (must be leashed in developed areas)

#### Missouri State Regulations

**Alcohol:**
- Legal on rivers, but responsible consumption only
- Disposable containers encouraged to be non-glass
- Don't drink and paddle irresponsibly

**Fishing License:**
- Required for anglers 16-64 (with exceptions)
- Available online at mdc.mo.gov
- Daily limits apply (check current regulations)

**Littering:**
- Illegal and carries fines
- Pack out ALL trash, even biodegradables

**Private Property:**
- Much riverfront is private
- Respect "No Trespassing" signs
- Stay on gravel bars or public access points

### Step 9: Best Times to Float

#### Spring (March - May)

**Pros:**
- Highest water levels (faster current, easier navigation)
- Wildflowers blooming
- Fewer crowds
- Cooler temperatures

**Cons:**
- Cold water (hypothermia risk)
- Unpredictable weather
- Some access points may be muddy
- Spring rains can cause flooding

**Best for:** Experienced paddlers, those who don't mind cold water

#### Summer (June - August)

**Pros:**
- Warmest water temperatures (perfect for swimming)
- Consistent weather (mostly sunny)
- Peak season - all outfitters operating
- Long daylight hours

**Cons:**
- Crowded (especially weekends)
- Hottest temperatures (heat exhaustion risk)
- Lower water levels (may scrape bottom in spots)
- Higher prices for rentals

**Best for:** Beginners, families, first-time floaters

**Timing tips:**
- Float weekdays to avoid crowds
- Start early to beat the heat
- July-August has lowest water levels

#### Fall (September - November)

**Pros:**
- Spectacular fall foliage (peak: mid-October)
- Cooler comfortable temperatures
- Fewer crowds
- Lower rental prices

**Cons:**
- Shorter daylight hours
- Colder water
- Some outfitters close after Labor Day
- Weather more unpredictable

**Best for:** Experienced floaters, photographers, solitude-seekers

#### Winter (December - February)

**Generally not recommended** for recreational floating:
- Very cold water (severe hypothermia risk)
- Most outfitters closed
- Unpredictable weather and ice
- Only for expert paddlers with cold-water gear

### Bonus Tips from Experienced Floaters

1. **Start small:** Your first trip should be a day float, not a multi-day expedition.

2. **Go with experienced people if possible:** Learn from others before leading your own trip.

3. **Invest in good water shoes:** Your feet will thank you. Tevas, Chacos, or Keens are worth it.

4. **Bring more water than you think you need:** Dehydration sneaks up on you.

5. **Reapply sunscreen religiously:** River sun is intense due to reflection.

6. **Take a dry bag trash bag:** Makes cleanup easier and protects environment.

7. **Bring a small anchor:** Helpful for lunch/swim stops in current.

8. **Pack a small repair kit:** Duct tape, extra cord, zip ties for equipment fixes.

9. **Use Float Planner before your trip:** Visualize your route, plan times, find campsites at floatplanner.com

10. **Arrive early, leave late:** Best camping spots are claimed early; late departures are more relaxed.

11. **Expect to get wet:** Even if you don't tip, splashing and rain happen.

12. **Don't overpack:** Space in canoes/kayaks is limited. Be selective.

13. **Bring games and entertainment:** Evenings at camp can be long without screens.

14. **Watch the water level:** Too low = scraping and portaging. Too high = fast current and danger.

15. **Talk to locals and outfitters:** They know current conditions and hidden spots.
