## What Exactly is a Float Trip?

If you've never been on a float trip, the concept is beautifully simple: you climb into a raft, canoe, kayak, or inner tube at one point on the river, and the current carries you downstream to another point where a shuttle picks you up. The journey might last 2 hours or 2 days, depending on your route.

Unlike whitewater rafting out West, Missouri float trips are primarily relaxing, scenic experiences. The spring-fed rivers maintain a steady flow year-round, with water temperatures hovering between 58-63¬∞F even in summer‚Äîmaking them perfect for cooling off on hot Midwest days.

**What makes Missouri floating unique:**

- **Spring-fed clarity:** Most Missouri float streams are fed by underground springs, creating vodka-clear water where you can see fish swimming beneath your boat
- **Natural beauty:** Limestone bluffs, caves, waterfalls, and dense Ozark forests line the banks
- **Accessibility:** Numerous outfitters handle all logistics (rentals, shuttles, camping) so beginners don't need any equipment
- **Social atmosphere:** Gravel bar stops for swimming, picnicking, and socializing are part of the culture
- **Year-round availability:** While summer is most popular, fall and spring offer fewer crowds and stunning foliage

The pace is leisurely‚Äîyou'll paddle occasionally to steer and navigate slower sections, but the current does most of the work. This is about disconnecting from daily stress and reconnecting with nature, friends, and family.

## Choosing Your First River: A Beginner's Guide

Not all Missouri rivers are created equal, and choosing the right one for your first trip makes all the difference. Here's how to pick your perfect starter river.

### Best Rivers for First-Time Floaters

#### **1. Meramec River (Near St. Louis)**

**Why it's great for beginners:** The Meramec is Missouri's most accessible float river, located just 90 minutes from St. Louis. The upper sections offer gentle Class I water perfect for first-timers, with numerous outfitters, established infrastructure, and shorter float options.

- **Difficulty:** Easy (Class I)
- **Popular sections:** 4-10 mile trips from various access points
- **Best for:** Day-trippers, families, groups who want a party atmosphere
- **Season:** May through September (water levels drop in late summer)
- **Location:** Steelville area‚Äîdubbed the "Float Capital of Missouri"

**Recommended outfitters:** Bass River Resort, Blue Springs Ranch, Ozark Outdoors Riverfront Resort

**Note:** The Meramec can get crowded on summer weekends, especially near Steelville. Float midweek for a quieter experience.

#### **2. Current River (Southern Missouri)**

**Why it's great for beginners:** As part of the Ozark National Scenic Riverways, the Current River is arguably Missouri's most beautiful float stream. The upper sections feature gentle current, spectacular scenery, and unique attractions like Cave Spring‚Äîa cave you can paddle 150 feet inside.

- **Difficulty:** Easy to Moderate (Class I with occasional Class II sections)
- **Popular sections:** Akers Ferry to Pulltite (10 miles, 5-6 hours)
- **Best for:** Nature lovers, photographers, anyone seeking pristine wilderness
- **Season:** Year-round (spring-fed maintains consistent levels)
- **Location:** Salem to Eminence area

**Recommended outfitters:** Akers Ferry Canoe Rental, Current River Canoe Rental, Carr's Canoe Rental

**Insider tip:** The Current River stays 58-63¬∞F year-round, so it's refreshingly cold even in July heat‚Äîplan accordingly.

#### **3. Elk River (Southwest Missouri)**

**Why it's great for beginners:** Crystal-clear water, numerous gravel bars, and a family-friendly atmosphere make the Elk River perfect for first-timers. The river features gentle current with plenty of shallow swimming areas where kids can play while parents relax on the bank.

- **Difficulty:** Very Easy (Class I)
- **Popular sections:** 4-8 mile trips
- **Best for:** Families with children, first-time paddlers, casual tubers
- **Season:** May through September
- **Location:** Noel/Pineville area

**Recommended outfitters:** Big Elk Camp & Canoe, Eagles Nest Camp and Canoe, River Ranch Resort

**Family bonus:** Many Elk River outfitters offer "loop" tube floats where parents can watch from the bank‚Äîperfect for young kids.

#### **4. Niangua River (Central Missouri)**

**Why it's great for beginners:** Warm, calm water with a gentle current and short float options make the Niangua ideal for nervous first-timers. The river is also famous for trout fishing, so you'll see anglers casting from the banks.

- **Difficulty:** Very Easy (Class I)
- **Popular sections:** 4-7 mile trips
- **Best for:** Beginners, families, combination floating/fishing trips
- **Season:** May through September
- **Location:** Near Camdenton/Lake of the Ozarks area

**Recommended outfitters:** Niangua River Oasis (NRO), Sand Spring Resort and Canoe

### Rivers to Save for Later

While Missouri has many fantastic rivers, some are better suited for your *second* or *third* float trip after you've gained experience:

- **Big Piney River:** More remote with longer stretches between access points‚Äîbetter for experienced paddlers
- **Jacks Fork River:** Beautiful but can have challenging low-water sections and longer required trips
- **Eleven Point River:** Wilderness character with fewer services and longer floats
- **Gasconade River:** One of Missouri's longest rivers with strong current in some sections

**Pro tip:** Start with a 4-6 hour float on a beginner-friendly river. You can always graduate to longer, more challenging adventures once you've mastered the basics.

## Understanding River Difficulty: Class Ratings Explained

Missouri rivers use the International Scale of River Difficulty to rate their challenges. Here's what you need to know:

### **Class I (Easy):**
- Moving water with small waves
- Few or no obstructions
- **Examples:** Most sections of Meramec, Niangua, and Elk rivers
- **Beginner-friendly?** Yes‚Äîperfect for first-timers

### **Class II (Novice):**
- Straightforward rapids with wide, clear channels
- Occasional maneuvering required
- **Examples:** Sections of Current River, some Meramec stretches
- **Beginner-friendly?** Yes, with basic paddling skills

### **Class III and above:**
- Not common on Missouri float streams
- Requires experience and strong paddling skills
- **Beginner-friendly?** No‚Äîsave these for later

**Bottom line:** Stick to Class I rivers for your first trip. Even Class II sections are manageable for beginners in rafts or kayaks with proper instruction from your outfitter.

## The Essential Gear List: What to Bring (and What to Leave Home)

One of the biggest mistakes first-timers make is either over-packing or under-packing. Here's exactly what you need.

### **What Outfitters Provide:**
- Canoe, kayak, raft, or tube rental
- Paddles (for canoes/kayaks)
- Life jackets (PFDs) for all passengers
- Dry bags or trash bags
- Shuttle service (to and from river access points)

### **Essential Items to Bring:**

#### **Safety & Sun Protection:**
- **Sunscreen (reef-safe, waterproof SPF 30+)** ‚Äì You'll burn faster than you think with sun reflecting off water
- **Wide-brim hat or visor** ‚Äì Protects face and neck
- **Polarized sunglasses with strap** ‚Äì Reduces glare; strap prevents loss
- **Bug spray** ‚Äì Mosquitoes love gravel bar breaks
- **First aid kit** ‚Äì For cuts, scrapes, blisters

#### **Hydration & Snacks:**
- **Large cooler (40+ quarts)** ‚Äì For all-day floats; get one that floats if possible
- **1 gallon of water per person minimum** ‚Äì River water is NOT drinkable despite clarity
- **Electrolyte drinks** ‚Äì Gatorade, Liquid IV, etc.
- **Easy-to-eat snacks** ‚Äì Sandwiches, PB&J, trail mix, crackers, fruit
- **Ice** ‚Äì Keep cooler contents cold; bags work better than blocks

**Alcohol note:** Beer and alcoholic beverages are allowed on most Missouri rivers, BUT glass containers and Styrofoam coolers are prohibited. Use cans and hard-sided coolers only.

#### **Footwear:**
- **Water shoes or sport sandals with straps** ‚Äì River bottoms are rocky; flip-flops will float away
- **NO barefoot floating** ‚Äì Risk of cuts from submerged branches, broken glass, sharp rocks

#### **Clothing:**
- **Swimsuit** ‚Äì Obviously
- **Quick-dry shirt (sun shirt/rash guard)** ‚Äì Long sleeves prevent sunburn
- **Change of clothes in waterproof bag** ‚Äì For the ride home
- **Towel** ‚Äì For after the float

#### **Tech & Valuables:**
- **Waterproof phone case or GoPro** ‚Äì Standard phone cases aren't enough; get a proper waterproof case
- **Waterproof dry bag** ‚Äì For keys, wallet, phone (20L size recommended)
- **Portable phone charger** ‚Äì Charged beforehand, sealed in dry bag

#### **Optional but Recommended:**
- **Small rope (20-30 feet)** ‚Äì For tying boats together during gravel bar stops (DO NOT tie while floating‚Äîit's illegal and dangerous)
- **Dry bag backpack** ‚Äì Easier to carry than traditional dry bags
- **Binoculars** ‚Äì For wildlife watching
- **Fishing gear** ‚Äì If you have a valid Missouri fishing license

### **What NOT to Bring:**

‚ùå **Glass containers** ‚Äì Prohibited; subject to fines  
‚ùå **Styrofoam coolers** ‚Äì Prohibited; break apart and pollute  
‚ùå **Expensive jewelry** ‚Äì You WILL lose it  
‚ùå **Drones** ‚Äì Many areas prohibit them  
‚ùå **Weapons** ‚Äì Check regulations; generally prohibited in national parks

## How River Shuttles Work: Logistics Made Simple

First-timers often stress about shuttle logistics, but outfitters make this incredibly easy. Here's exactly how it works:

### **Step 1: Arrive at the Outfitter**
- Arrive at the outfitter's location (usually a campground or main office)
- Check in, pay for rentals, sign waivers
- Use bathrooms, fill water bottles, apply sunscreen
- Load your cooler and gear into the provided boats

### **Step 2: Shuttle to Put-In**
- The outfitter loads boats onto trailers
- You ride in a shuttle bus/truck to the upstream launch point (put-in)
- Your car stays at the outfitter's location

### **Step 3: Float Downstream**
- Launch at the put-in with your group
- Float for the agreed-upon distance (usually 3-8 hours depending on river section)
- The outfitter tells you exactly what the take-out looks like and how to recognize it

### **Step 4: Take-Out and Return**
- Watch for the designated take-out point (usually marked with signs)
- Pull your boat out of the river at the gravel bar or boat ramp
- Wait for the outfitter's shuttle to pick you up
- Ride back to the outfitter's location where your car is parked

### **Important Shuttle Tips:**

‚úÖ **Clarify the take-out point** ‚Äì Ask your outfitter to describe landmarks: "After you pass the big bluff with the cave opening, look for our blue sign on the right bank"

‚úÖ **Download offline maps** ‚Äì Cell service is spotty on rivers; download Google Maps offline for the area before you leave

‚úÖ **Don't miss your take-out** ‚Äì If you accidentally float past it, pull over and call the outfitter; they'll come get you

‚úÖ **Timing is approximate** ‚Äì Outfitters give estimated float times, but your actual time depends on water levels, how often you stop, and your paddling speed

‚úÖ **Shuttles run on schedules** ‚Äì Some outfitters do pickup rounds every 2 hours; ask about their schedule

**Common mistake:** First-timers sometimes park at public access points and try to arrange their own shuttles. Don't do this‚Äîpay the extra $10-15 per person and let the outfitter handle logistics. It's worth every penny.

## What to Expect on the Water: A Hour-by-Hour Breakdown

Let's walk through a typical 6-hour float trip so you know exactly what to expect:

### **Hour 1: The Launch (9:00 AM - 10:00 AM)**

You'll feel nervous and maybe a little clumsy getting into the boat. That's normal. Outfitters usually give a quick 5-minute tutorial:

- How to get in/out without tipping
- Basic paddle strokes (forward, backward, turning)
- What to do if you tip over (stay calm, stay with the boat, float feet-first downstream)

The first mile is the learning curve. You'll probably:
- Spin in circles a few times
- Bump into rocks or branches
- Maybe tip over (it happens‚Äîlaugh it off)

**Tip:** The person in the back of the canoe does most of the steering. If you're with an experienced paddler, let them take the stern (back) position.

### **Hour 2-3: Finding Your Rhythm (10:00 AM - 12:00 PM)**

By now you've figured out how to paddle without fighting your partner. The river does most of the work‚Äîyou're just making minor course corrections.

This is when you start to relax and enjoy:
- Wildlife spotting (herons, turtles, fish jumping, maybe river otters)
- Chatting with your group
- Taking photos
- Cracking open that first cold beverage

You'll encounter other floaters. River etiquette says you greet them with a friendly wave or "hello!"

### **Hour 3-4: Gravel Bar Break (12:00 PM - 2:00 PM)**

Around midday, pull over at a gravel bar for lunch. This is peak float trip culture:

- Drag boats onto the gravel bar
- Set up a makeshift picnic
- Go for a swim in the clear, cold water
- Kids build rock towers and hunt for crayfish
- Adults relax in camp chairs (if you brought them)

**Duration:** Most groups stop for 30-60 minutes. Don't rush‚Äîthis is part of the experience.

**Gravel bar etiquette:**
- Don't blast music (some people come for peace and quiet)
- Pack out ALL trash‚Äîleave no trace
- Respect others' space; don't set up right next to another group if the bar is large
- If nature calls, walk far downstream into the woods; many gravel bars have no facilities

### **Hour 4-5: The Afternoon Stretch (2:00 PM - 4:00 PM)**

Post-lunch, the sun is at its peak. This is when sunburns happen if you didn't reapply sunscreen.

The river might get busier if you're on a popular stream on a summer weekend. You'll see:
- Large groups with floating coolers
- Tubers moving slowly (tubes are slower than paddled boats)
- Maybe a park ranger checking for life jacket compliance and trash violations

**Stay alert for:**
- Low-hanging tree branches (they'll smack you if you're not paying attention)
- Shallow sections where you need to get out and walk the boat
- Logjams or strainers (avoid these‚Äînever paddle into blocked channels)

### **Hour 5-6: The Home Stretch (4:00 PM - 5:00 PM)**

You're getting tired now. Arms are sore, maybe a little sunburned, but you're also proud‚Äîyou're doing it!

Start watching for your take-out point. Outfitters usually describe it clearly: "You'll see a big bluff on the left, then our sign is 100 yards downstream on the right bank."

**How to recognize take-outs:**
- Look for outfitter signs or flags
- Gravel bar boat ramps
- Groups of boats pulled up waiting for shuttles

**Taking out:**
- Paddle toward the bank slowly
- As your boat touches gravel, one person steps out carefully and pulls the boat onto shore
- Unload your gear
- Pull the boat completely out of the water
- Wait for the shuttle

### **The Ride Back:**

You'll be tired, maybe a little buzzed, definitely sunburned (hopefully not too badly). The shuttle ride back is full of:
- Recounting the best moments
- Laughing about who tipped over
- Planning when to do it again

**Back at the outfitter:** Turn in life jackets, paddles, and boats. Load up your car. Change into dry clothes if you're smart.

**Congratulations‚Äîyou're officially a Missouri river floater!**

## River Etiquette: The Unwritten Rules

Missouri float culture has evolved over generations. Here's how to be a respectful floater:

### **The Golden Rules:**

**1. Pack It In, Pack It Out**
- Bring a trash bag and use it
- **Never** leave cans, bottles, wrappers, or food waste on gravel bars
- Fines for littering can exceed $500

**2. Respect Private Property**
- Much of Missouri's riverfront is privately owned
- Gravel bars below the high-water mark are public; banks and bluffs are often private
- Don't trespass, camp, or build fires on private land without permission

**3. Life Jackets Are Non-Negotiable**
- Missouri law requires life jackets for children under 7
- Even strong swimmers should wear them‚Äîrivers are unpredictable
- You can be fined for not having enough PFDs for everyone in your boat

**4. Keep Noise Reasonable**
- Music on gravel bars is fine at moderate volume
- Screaming, shouting, and amplified music on the water disturbs wildlife and other floaters
- Many people come to rivers for peace‚Äîrespect that

**5. Don't Tie Boats Together While Floating**
- It's illegal in Missouri and dangerous
- Tied boats can't maneuver around obstacles and can flip easily
- You will be fined if caught

**6. Give Anglers Space**
- Fishermen have as much right to the river as floaters
- When you see someone fishing, paddle wide around their lines
- Keep conversations quiet when passing anglers

**7. No Glass on the River**
- It's prohibited on most Missouri streams
- Broken glass causes injuries and doesn't decompose
- Use cans or plastic bottles only

**8. Yield to Downstream Traffic**
- Boats coming downstream have the right of way
- If you're stopped or moving slowly, watch upstream for faster traffic

### **Bathroom Breaks in the Wild:**

Let's address the uncomfortable question: *What if I need to go #2 on a multi-hour float?*

**For pee breaks:**
- Find a secluded spot downstream from where people are swimming
- Women: Pull your boat into a quiet cove and squat in knee-deep water (discreet and practical)
- Men: Same‚Äîfind privacy away from crowds

**For solid waste:**
- If possible, wait until you're back at the outfitter
- If you can't wait: walk far into the woods (200+ feet from water), dig a 6-inch hole with a stick, bury waste and toilet paper
- **Never** go directly into the river

**Pro tip:** Plan your float to coincide with outfitter facilities. Many multi-day routes have campgrounds with bathrooms midway.

## Safety First: What Every Beginner Needs to Know

Missouri float trips are generally very safe, but rivers are still wild environments. Follow these guidelines:

### **Before You Launch:**

‚úÖ **Check river conditions** ‚Äì Visit [Missouri DNR's River Levels page](https://www.nps.gov/ozar/planyourvisit/levels.htm) or ask your outfitter about water levels

‚úÖ **File a float plan** ‚Äì Tell someone not on the trip where you're going, when you'll be back, and who's in your group

‚úÖ **Know your limits** ‚Äì Shorter floats (3-4 hours) are smarter for beginners than all-day marathons

‚úÖ **Check the weather** ‚Äì Thunderstorms can cause flash flooding; never float with storms in the forecast

### **While Floating:**

‚úÖ **Wear your life jacket at all times** ‚Äì Even strong swimmers can be incapacitated by hitting their head on a rock

‚úÖ **Stay with your boat if you capsize** ‚Äì Hold onto the upstream side; let the river carry you to a shallow area or gravel bar

‚úÖ **Float feet-first if swimming** ‚Äì Never go headfirst; you'll see obstacles coming and can push off with your feet

‚úÖ **Avoid strainers and logjams** ‚Äì These are trees/branches blocking the current; they can trap boats and people‚Äîpaddle around them with wide berth

‚úÖ **Watch for low-hanging branches** ‚Äì Called "sweepers," they can knock you out of your boat

‚úÖ **Stay hydrated** ‚Äì Drink water regularly; dehydration sneaks up on you

‚úÖ **Buddy system** ‚Äì Never float alone; always stay within sight of your group

### **What to Do If Things Go Wrong:**

**If you capsize:**
1. Stay calm‚Äîdon't panic
2. Hold onto your boat on the upstream side
3. Keep your feet pointed downstream (feet-first floating position)
4. Float with the current to a shallow area or gravel bar
5. If the boat is swamped with water, drag it to shore and dump it

**If someone is injured:**
1. Get them to shore immediately
2. Provide first aid if trained
3. Send someone downstream to the nearest outfitter or access point for help
4. Call 911 if you have cell service (spotty on most rivers)

**If you get separated from your group:**
1. Pull over at the next gravel bar and wait
2. Your group should float past looking for you
3. If lost, continue to the designated take-out point; your outfitter will find you

**If you float past your take-out:**
1. Pull over at the next gravel bar
2. Try calling your outfitter (if you have service)
3. Wait; outfitters regularly patrol for missed take-outs

## Choosing the Right Watercraft: Canoe vs. Kayak vs. Raft vs. Tube

Your choice of boat affects your experience significantly. Here's how to choose:

### **Canoes**

**Best for:** Couples, friends who want to paddle together, families with kids

**Pros:**
- Stable and roomy (can fit a cooler between paddlers)
- Two people share paddling effort
- Easier to get in/out than kayaks
- Shallow draft works well in low water

**Cons:**
- Requires coordination between paddlers (source of many couple's arguments!)
- More affected by wind than kayaks
- Easier to tip if you don't distribute weight evenly

**Beginner-friendly?** Yes, but bring patience if you're paddling with a partner

### **Kayaks**

**Best for:** Solo paddlers, couples who want separate boats, people with paddling experience

**Pros:**
- Easy to maneuver
- Fast and responsive
- Low profile = less affected by wind
- You control your own destiny

**Cons:**
- Less storage space for coolers/gear
- Harder to get in/out (especially for older folks or those with mobility issues)
- Can feel tippy at first

**Beginner-friendly?** Yes, especially sit-on-top kayaks (more stable than sit-inside)

### **Rafts**

**Best for:** Large groups, families with small children, parties, total beginners

**Pros:**
- Extremely stable (nearly impossible to tip)
- Fits 4-8 people plus coolers
- Great for socializing
- Kids can move around safely

**Cons:**
- Slower than canoes/kayaks
- Harder to steer precisely
- Can get stuck on shallow sections
- Less "authentic" paddling experience

**Beginner-friendly?** Absolutely‚Äîthe most beginner-friendly option

### **Tubes**

**Best for:** Short floats, warm summer days, people who want to relax

**Pros:**
- Zero skill required
- Ultimate relaxation
- Cheap rentals ($10-20)
- Perfect for hot weather

**Cons:**
- SLOW (takes 2x longer than paddled boats)
- No steering control (you go where the river takes you)
- More sun exposure (no shade)
- Not suitable for longer trips (your butt will hurt)

**Beginner-friendly?** Yes, but only for short 2-3 hour trips

### **First-Timer Recommendations:**

- **Solo beginner:** Kayak (sit-on-top)
- **Couple:** Raft or two separate kayaks (trust me on this)
- **Family with kids under 10:** Raft
- **Group of 4-6 friends:** 2-3 canoes
- **Party atmosphere:** Rafts and tubes

## When to Go: Seasonal Guide to Missouri Floating

Missouri rivers are accessible year-round, but each season offers different experiences:

**Water Levels:** High from spring runoff  
**Temperatures:** Air 70-80¬∞F, water 60-65¬∞F

**Water Levels:** Moderate to low

**Water Levels:** Low to moderate  
**Temperatures:** Air 70-80¬∞F, water 58-65¬∞F  
**Crowds:** Light

- Spectacular fall foliage (late Sept-Oct)

- Comfortable air temps
- Lower prices

**Cons:**
- Water too cold for extended swimming
- Shorter daylight (plan earlier starts)
- Some outfitters close after Labor Day

**Water Levels:** Variable (can be high with flooding)  
**Temperatures:** Air 40-60¬∞F, water 55-60¬∞F  
**Crowds:** Minimal  

**Pros:**
- Absolute solitude
- Bare trees reveal bluff formations

**Cons:**
- COLD water = hypothermia risk
- Limited outfitter services
- Shorter days
- Wetsuits recommended

**Best for:** Experienced paddlers, anglers, solitude-seekers with proper gear

### **Best Time for First-Timers:**

**Late June through early September, weekdays if possible.** You'll get warm weather, manageable water levels, and full outfitter services.

## Top 5 Mistakes First-Timers Make (And How to Avoid Them)

Learn from others' mistakes:

### **1. Not Applying Enough Sunscreen**

**The mistake:** "I'll just put some on before we launch."

**Why it fails:** You're on the water 5-8 hours with sun reflecting from above AND below. You WILL burn.

**The fix:** Apply 30 minutes before launching, reapply every 90 minutes, use SPF 30+ waterproof formula, don't forget ears, tops of feet, and backs of knees

### **2. Wearing the Wrong Shoes**

**The mistake:** Flip-flops or going barefoot.

**Why it fails:** Flip-flops float away within the first hour. Bare feet get cut on rocks, broken glass, and sharp sticks.

**The fix:** Water shoes or sport sandals with heel straps. Brands like Chaco, Teva, or Keen are perfect.

### **3. Bringing Too Much Stuff**

**The mistake:** Packing like you're going on a week-long camping trip.

**Why it fails:** Limited boat space, everything gets wet, you'll lose half of it in the river.

**The fix:** One waterproof dry bag with essentials. Cooler with food/drinks. That's it.

### **4. Not Checking Water Levels**

**The mistake:** Showing up without knowing if the river is too high, too low, or just right.

**Why it fails:** Low water = constantly scraping on rocks, getting stuck. High water = dangerous current, rapids too strong for beginners.

**The fix:** Check [USGS water levels](https://waterdata.usgs.gov/mo/nwis/current/?type=flow) or call the outfitter 24 hours before. If levels are high or low, they'll recommend alternate rivers.

### **5. Fighting with Your Canoe Partner**

**The mistake:** "You're paddling wrong!" "No, YOU'RE paddling wrong!"

**Why it fails:** Canoes require coordination. Arguing makes it worse.

**The fix:** 
- Front paddler sets the pace; back paddler steers and matches rhythm
- Decide beforehand who's in charge of navigation
- When in doubt, stop paddling and let the current do the work
- Or just get separate kayaks

**Mammals:**

**Reptiles & Amphibians:**
- Painted turtles (sunning on logs)

### **Snakes: What You Need to Know**

Missouri rivers are home to both harmless water snakes and venomous species. Here's how to stay safe:

**Venomous species to watch for:**
- **Cottonmouths (Water Moccasins):** Thick-bodied, dark colored, white mouth interior when threatened
- **Copperheads:** Hourglass pattern, less common near water

**Harmless species (often mistaken for cottonmouths):**
- Northern water snakes (most common)
- Diamondback water snakes

**Safety tips:**
- Snakes are more afraid of you than you are of them
- Most bites occur when people try to handle or kill snakes‚ÄîDON'T
- Watch for snakes on low-hanging branches (they sun themselves there)
- Look before stepping out of the boat
- If you see a snake, give it space and paddle away calmly
- Clear water helps you see them coming

**Reality check:** Thousands of people float Missouri rivers every year. Snake encounters are rare, and bites are even rarer. Don't let fear of snakes keep you from enjoying the river.

## FAQs: Answers to Every Beginner's Questions

**Q: Do I need any experience to go on a float trip?**  
A: Nope! Missouri float trips are designed for complete beginners. Outfitters give basic instruction, and the gentle current does most of the work.

**Q: Can I bring my dog?**  
A: Most outfitters allow dogs, but check first. Dogs must wear life jackets and stay leashed. Be prepared to clean up after them.

**Q: What if I can't swim?**  
A: You'll wear a life jacket the entire time, so non-swimmers are safe. Stick to rafts or tandem canoes with an experienced paddler.

**Q: Are there bathrooms on the river?**  
A: Only at access points and some campgrounds. Plan accordingly‚Äîyou'll likely need to use nature.

**Q: How do I keep my phone dry?**  
A: Use a proper waterproof case (not just "water-resistant") or leave it in a sealed dry bag. Better yet, bring a GoPro instead.

**Q: What if it rains?**  
A: Light rain is fine‚Äîyou're already wet! Heavy rain or thunderstorms are dangerous (lightning, flash flooding). Check weather before launching and postpone if storms are forecast.

**Q: Can I bring marijuana/CBD?**  
A: Cannabis is illegal in Missouri for recreational use. Don't bring it on the river‚Äîrangers patrol and will cite you.

**Q: Will my valuables be safe in my car?**  
A: Most outfitters have secure parking, but don't leave valuables visible. Lock them in your trunk.

**Q: How physically demanding is floating?**  
A: Not very. You paddle occasionally to steer, but the current does most of the work. Average fitness is fine.

**Q: Are there alligators in Missouri rivers?**  
A: No! Alligators don't live this far north. You're safe from gators.
